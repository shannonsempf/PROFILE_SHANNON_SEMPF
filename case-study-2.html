Case Study Context

The Department of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries (DAFF) is a government department in the state of Queensland, Australia. DAFF is responsible for supporting and developing the state's agricultural, fisheries, and forestry industries. Its main purpose is to promote sustainable practices, ensure food safety and biosecurity, support rural communities, and enhance the economic growth and competitiveness of Queensland's primary industries.

The Problem

Effective communication is crucial in order for the department to achieve its role of supporting and developing the states agricultural fisheries, and forestry industries. Presently, one of the primary means of communication between the Department of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries and its constituents is via the DAFF Queensland website. As it is the deperments primary means of communication it is imperative that this website is inviting, informative and user friendly.

The Goal

Facilitate better communication between the DAFF and its constituents by identifyiong issues with the current website design, and re-creating an intuitive website that allows users to locate the information they need more quickly and easily.

User Research
User research was completed prior to re-designing the DAFF website in order to identify:
  • What aspects of the current website might be hindering users from finding the information they need with ease
  • What areas would be best to focus on in the re-design of the website
  • What aspects of the website that may be improved in order for the department to better communicate with their stakeholders.

Summary of research findings
1. Poor information architecture and navigation
Users found the current information architecture and navigation layout to be confusing and inefficient. When tested many users found that it took a long time and large number of clicks to find the information they were looking for.
2. Inefficient homepage layout
Currently a large proportion of the home page is dedicated to blank space or information that our users thought to be of little importance.While some aspects of the current website were useful to users (such as the search bar, ‘contacts’ and 'frequently viewed pages' sections) these sections were often hard to find and took up significantly smaller sections of the homepage than other, less important assets.
3. Lack of clarity and direction
Overall many users found the purpose of the DAFF and the website to be unclear. Many testers questioned who the DAFF was and what kind of information they might find on the site.

The Solution

The goal was to facilitate better communication between the DAFF and its constituents by identifyiong issues with the current website design, and re-creating an intuitive website that allows users to locate the information they need more quickly and easily.
This goal will be delivered by:
  1. Improving the ease at which users can find information on the website by restructuring the websites information architecture and navigation.
  2. Reorganizing the website to prioritize the most useful and relevant content, while minimising dead space.
  3. Making the purpose of ‘The Department of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries (DAFF)’ and the role of the website clear.

Style Guide
Colour Pallet
Earthy tones, including shades of brown, beige, terracotta, olive green and soft grey were used for this website re-design. These tones were selected as they mimic the colours found in natural elements (such as soil, wood, plants and stone) which are synonymous with the agricultural and forestry industries.
Typography
Martel was chosen as the primary text for the website due to its simple, professional and readable design. Clear and simple typography such as Martel enhances communication and the user experience by ensuring that the text is easy to read and understand.
Accessibility
All button/text and background text combinations were tested to ensure acessability standards were met.
Responsive design / 12 column grid system

A column system was utilised in the design to allow the webpage to be easily altered to suit variable screen sizes. “large webpages followed a 12 column grid.

Limitations and Future considerations
Due to time constraints, and the fact that this was a solo task, the redesign of this website was largely limited to the homepage, navigation and information architecture, as these were identified as the most impactful design elements. Given greater time and resources, it would be beneficial for the redesign to extend to further areas of the website, such as secondary and tertiary pages.
